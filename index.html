1. Advanced Letter Theory

A (Anchor): Stability. Sets the "home" sound of your verse.

B (Balance): Contrast. Breaks the pattern to create tension or a "response."

X (Ghost Line): An unrhymed line. AXB creates a "cliffhanger" effect where the ear expects a rhyme but gets a statement instead, forcing focus onto the lyrics.

O (Internal Rhyme): Rhyming inside a line (e.g., "The light in the night is bright") increases speed and rhythmic energy.

2. Rhyme Math

Perfect Rhymes: (Sky/Fly) High resolution. Good for simple Pop hooks.

Slant Rhymes: (Stone/Home) Modern feel. Avoids sounding like a nursery rhyme.

Multisyllabic: (Vibration/Creation) Rhythmic complexity. Used often in Rap to create a "galloping" flow.

The Songwriting Bible

Close

1. The 3-Act Structure


Act 1 (Verse 1): The Setup. Use sensory details to show us where we are. Don't reveal the emotion yet.

Act 2 (Verse 2): The Conflict. What changed? Introduce the "But" or the "However."

Act 3 (Bridge): The Revelation. A new perspective, a secret, or a shift in time (past/future).

2. Prosody & Syllable Math

Prosody is the marriage of word meaning and musical rhythm. If your lyrics are about "falling," use descending syllable counts (8-6-4-2).

Stability: Match syllable counts (8-8-8-8) to make the listener feel safe.

Instability: Break the pattern (8-8-8-3) to create a "drop" that leads into a Chorus.

3. Sensory Anchoring (VAK)

Avoid abstract feelings like "I am lonely." Use Visual (flickering bulb), Auditory (humming fridge), and Kinesthetic (cold floor) details to anchor the listener's brain.

4. Object Writing

Take one object (a key, a watch, a door) and write for 5 minutes using only the 5 senses. This generates unique metaphors that nobody else has used.
